## 1. Introduction

### Purpose

This guide is **not** a translation manual. Its purpose is to preserve the editorial identity of Fabrizio's brand while adapting communication naturally for English-speaking audiences.

### Why Voice Consistency Matters

Every piece of content — whether a website, podcast, article, newsletter, or social media post — is an extension of the same conversation. Inconsistent voice creates friction. Consistent voice builds trust.

This document ensures that every future collaborator, editor, or writer can contribute content that feels unmistakably like Fabrizio's brand.

### Scope

This guide applies to all English-language communications:

- Website content
- Podcast episodes and descriptions
- Articles and essays
- Newsletter communications
- Workshop and retreat materials
- Online course content
- Community communications
- Video scripts
- Social media
- Future publications

### Core Principle

**The English voice should never sound like a translation.**

It should sound as though the brand was originally created in English. Natural language always takes priority over literal translation.

## 2. Audience

### Who They Are

The ideal reader is a thoughtful adult, typically between 30 and 60 years old, who is:

- Educated and intellectually curious
- Interested in personal growth and self-understanding
- Drawn to contemplative practices but not necessarily religious
- Skeptical of quick fixes and spiritual clichés
- Seeking depth over entertainment

### What They Are Looking For

- Authentic guidance, not expert authority
- Space to reflect, not answers to memorize
- Companionship in their exploration, not instruction
- Practical wisdom, not abstract philosophy
- A voice that respects their intelligence

### Emotional State on Arrival

Most visitors arrive in one of these states:

- **Curious but uncertain** — drawn to the work but unsure if it is for them
- **Seeking calm** — overwhelmed by life and looking for stillness
- **In transition** — navigating change and seeking perspective
- **Tired of superficial solutions** — disappointed by self-help that promises too much

### Language They Distrust

- Corporate buzzwords ("leverage," "optimize," "unlock your potential")
- Spiritual clichés ("awaken your highest self," "divine timing")
- Marketing pressure ("limited time," "act now," "transform your life")
- Pseudo-scientific claims ("proven method," "guaranteed results")
- Guru language ("master," "enlightenment," "ancient secrets")

### Language That Makes Them Feel Safe

- Honest, grounded descriptions
- Invitations rather than commands
- Acknowledgment of complexity
- Humility over certainty
- Presence over promises
- Simple, clear sentences

### Expectations of Wellness Professionals

The English-speaking wellness audience expects:

- Professionalism without formality
- Warmth without false friendliness
- Expertise without superiority
- Clarity without oversimplification
- Depth without pretension

## 3. Brand Personality

### If the Brand Were a Person

The brand is:

**A calm companion on a contemplative journey.**

Not a teacher. Not a healer. Not a guru. A companion — someone who walks beside you, offering presence and perspective.

### Personality Traits

| Trait | What It Means |
|-------|---------------|
| **Calm** | Never rushed, never urgent. Speaks at a pace that invites breathing. |
| **Thoughtful** | Every word is considered. Nothing is casual or careless. |
| **Warm** | Welcoming without being effusive. Kind without being sentimental. |
| **Grounded** | Rooted in real experience, not abstract theory. |
| **Humble** | Speaks from experience, not authority. Admits what it does not know. |
| **Present** | Focused on what is here, now. Not chasing the next thing. |
| **Compassionate** | Understands struggle without trying to fix it. |
| **Patient** | Allows the reader time to arrive at their own understanding. |
| **Quietly confident** | Does not need to prove itself. Certainty without announcement. |
| **Authentic** | Never performs. Never pretends. |

### What the Brand Is Not

- Not a guru or master
- Not a healer or fixer
- Not a motivational speaker
- Not a corporate wellness provider
- Not a spiritual teacher
- Not a self-help system

## 6. Writing Principles

### Core Principles

1. **Prefer active voice.**
   - "We offer companionship" not "Companionship is offered."

2. **Prefer invitations over commands.**
   - "Explore what is possible" not "Start your transformation today."

3. **Prefer verbs over abstract nouns.**
   - "Learning to be present" not "The cultivation of presence."

4. **Avoid unnecessary adjectives.**
   - "A space for reflection" not "A beautiful, transformative, life-changing space."

5. **Avoid information overload.**
   - Leave breathing space. Not every idea needs to be explained.

6. **Respect the reader's intelligence.**
   - Do not over-explain. Trust the reader to understand.

7. **Never exaggerate.**
   - No superlatives. No promises that cannot be kept.

8. **Never pressure.**
   - No urgency. No scarcity tactics.

9. **Never promise transformation.**
   - Offer possibility, not guarantees.

10. **Never position Fabrizio as a guru.**
    - Companion, not authority. Guide, not master.

## 7. Sentence Style

### Paragraph Rhythm

- Short paragraphs — typically 1–3 sentences
- Space between paragraphs
- One idea per paragraph

### Sentence Length

- Varied — some short, some longer
- Average: 12–20 words
- Short sentences for emphasis
- Longer sentences for reflection

### Reading Pace

- Slow and contemplative
- Never rushed
- Pauses built into the structure

### Silence Between Ideas

White space is as important as words. Allow the reader time to absorb each idea before introducing the next.

### Use of Questions

Questions are used sparingly and thoughtfully:

- To invite reflection, not to lead
- To open space, not to demand answers
- Never rhetorical questions

### Use of Metaphors

Metaphors should be:

- Simple and accessible
- Drawn from nature and everyday experience
- Never mystical or esoteric

### Use of Repetition

Repetition is used deliberately:

- To create rhythm
- To emphasize key ideas
- Never accidentally

## 12. Editorial Rhythm

Like:

- A thoughtful essay
- A calm conversation
- A shared reflection
- A quiet walk together

Never like:

- A lecture
- A motivational speech
- A sales page
- A self-help manual
- A class
- A performance

### Pacing

- Slow enough to breathe
- Space between ideas
- Time to absorb before moving on
- No rushing to the next point

### Tone

- Contemplative, not preachy
- Inviting, not demanding
- Honest, not performative
- Simple, not simplistic
- Deep, not obscure

## 13. Calls To Action

### CTA Philosophy

Calls to action should feel like natural invitations, not marketing commands.

### Words to Avoid in CTAs

- Buy
- Purchase
- Enroll Now
- Act Now
- Limited Time
- Only Today
- Don't Miss Out
- Get Started
- Sign Up

### Preferred CTA Language

- Explore
- Discover
- Reflect
- Listen
- Read
- Begin
- Learn More
- Book a Session
- Let's Talk
- Join
- View Upcoming Dates
- Schedule a Conversation

### CTA Principles

1. **Use verbs that invite, not demand.**
2. **Avoid urgency.** There is no rush.
3. **Avoid scarcity.** Nothing is "limited."
4. **Avoid pressure.** The reader is free to choose.
5. **Keep it simple.** One clear action, not multiple options.

## 16. Cultural Adaptation

### Literal Translation vs. Adaptation

**Never translate literally.** Always adapt for cultural context.

Example:
- Spanish: "Acompañamiento consciente"
- Literal: "Conscious companionship"
- Adapted: "A conscious companionship for your inner journey"

### When to Simplify Concepts

Some concepts that work well in Spanish-speaking wellness contexts may need simplification for English-speaking audiences:

- "Reprogramación" → "Pattern Release" (more accessible)
- "Sanación interior" → avoid "healing" entirely; use "understanding" or "exploration"
- "Despertar de la consciencia" → avoid "awakening"; use "awareness" or "clarity"

### When to Avoid Spiritual Vocabulary

Words like "healing," "energy," "awakening," "consciousness," "manifestation," and "vibration" carry baggage in English-speaking markets:

- They can signal New Age thinking, which many educated readers distrust
- They can reduce credibility with skeptical audiences
- They can feel like clichés

**Use contemplative language instead:**
- "Understanding" instead of "healing"
- "Awareness" instead of "consciousness"
- "Practice" instead of "energy work"
- "Reflection" instead of "manifestation"

### When Contemplative Language Works

Contemplative language — calm, reflective, philosophical — works well across all English-speaking markets when:

- It is grounded in real experience
- It avoids spiritual clichés
- It respects the reader's intelligence
- It invites rather than instructs

### When Practical Language Works Better

Practical language works better when:

- Describing concrete offerings (sessions, courses, retreats)
- Explaining how something works
- Addressing skepticism
- Speaking to readers new to contemplative practice

## 17. Examples

### Good Editorial Style

**Describing a session:**

> "A space to explore what is present for you — thoughts, feelings, patterns, questions. Not to fix anything, but to understand more deeply."

**Describing a retreat:**

> "Time away from the noise. Days spent in silence, in nature, in the company of others who are also learning to be present."

**Describing the podcast:**

> "Reflections to listen to in stillness. Not answers, but questions worth sitting with."

**Describing inner work:**

> "We all carry stories about who we are and how the world works. Often, we did not choose these stories — we inherited them. This work is about seeing them clearly, so we can choose differently."

### What These Examples Demonstrate

- Short paragraphs
- Simple vocabulary
- No exaggeration
- Invitation, not command
- Honesty about what is (and is not) offered
- Respect for the reader's intelligence

## 19. Editorial Decision Framework

### How to Choose Between Two Valid Options

When two phrasings both seem acceptable, use these principles to decide:

#### 1. Prioritize Natural English Over Literal Translation

If the Spanish original uses a construction that sounds unnatural in English, adapt it.

**Example:**
- Spanish: "Un acompañamiento para tu viaje interior"
- Literal: "A companionship for your inner journey"
- Natural: "Conscious companionship for your inner journey"

#### 2. Preserve Emotional Intention Before Preserving Wording

The feeling matters more than the exact words.

**Example:**
- If a Spanish phrase conveys warmth but the literal translation sounds cold, find different words that convey the same warmth.

#### 3. Adapt Concepts When Necessary

Some concepts do not translate directly. Adapt them for the target culture.

**Example:**
- "Sanación" in Spanish wellness contexts often means deep inner work. In English, "healing" can sound New Age or medical. Consider "understanding," "exploration," or "inner work" instead.

#### 4. Never Sacrifice Clarity for Poetry

Beautiful language is valuable, but not at the cost of understanding.

**Example:**
- "The stillness beneath the noise of becoming" is poetic but unclear.
- "Finding calm beneath the noise of daily life" is clearer.

#### 5. Never Sacrifice Authenticity for Marketing

If something sounds like marketing, it is probably wrong for this brand.

**Example:**
- "Transform your life in 8 weeks" sounds like marketing.
- "A space to explore what transformation might mean" sounds authentic.

#### 6. Maintain Consistency Across Every Channel

The voice on the website should be the same voice in the podcast, newsletter, social media, and courses.

### Practical Decision Examples

**Example 1: "Pattern Release" vs. "Releasing Patterns"**

Both are valid. "Pattern Release" sounds more like a named offering. "Releasing Patterns" sounds more like a description. Choose based on context:

- As a section title: "Pattern Release" (cleaner)
- As a description: "A process for releasing patterns" (more natural)

**Example 2: "Book a Session" vs. "Schedule a Session"**

Both are valid. "Book" is more direct. "Schedule" is slightly more formal. For this brand:

- "Book a Session" works for clear CTAs
- "Schedule a Conversation" works for softer invitations

**Example 3: "Join the Community" vs. "Become Part of Our Community"**

Both are valid. "Join" is simpler and more direct. "Become Part of" is warmer but longer. For this brand:

- "Join the Community" (preferred — simpler)
- "Become Part of Our Community" (acceptable when warmth is prioritized)

## 20. Document Maintenance

### Who Should Maintain This Guide

This guide should be maintained by:

- Fabrizio (as the voice of the brand)
- Any editor or writer working on English content
- The brand's creative director or content strategist (if one exists)

### When It Should Be Updated

This guide should be reviewed and potentially updated:

- **Annually** — as a regular check-in
- **When launching new offerings** — to ensure new content aligns
- **When working with new collaborators** — to ensure they understand the voice
- **When the brand evolves** — the voice may deepen over time

### How Future Contributors Should Use It

1. **Read the full guide** before writing any content.
2. **Keep it open** while writing or editing.
3. **Use the checklist** (Section 18) before publishing.
4. **Refer to the decision framework** (Section 19) when uncertain.
5. **Ask questions** if something is unclear.

### Evaluating New Content

Before publishing, ask:

1. Does this sound like the brand described in this guide?
2. Would Fabrizio say it this way?
3. Does it pass the checklist?
4. Does it feel calm, warm, and authentic?

### Evolution

This document should evolve with the brand. As Fabrizio's work deepens, the voice may deepen with it. But the core principles — calm, authenticity, humility, warmth — should remain constant.

## Closing

This guide is not a set of rules to follow mechanically. It is a description of a voice — a way of being with words.

The goal is not perfection. The goal is authenticity.

When in doubt, return to the core question:

> Does this sound like a calm companion walking beside the reader?

If yes, it is right for this brand.

If no, it needs to be reconsidered.
